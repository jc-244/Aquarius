■ Example 1.9  
Show that the system described by the equation

$$
\frac{dy}{dt}+3y(t)=f(t)
$$
\hfill (1.43)

is linear.

Let the system response to the inputs $f_1(t)$ and $f_2(t)$ be $y_1(t)$ and $y_2(t)$, respectively. Then

$$
\frac{dy_1}{dt}+3y_1(t)=f_1(t)
$$

and

$$
\frac{dy_2}{dt}+3y_2(t)=f_2(t)
$$

Multiplying the first equation by $k_1$, the second with $k_2$, and adding them yields

$$
\frac{d}{dt}[k_1y_1(t)+k_2y_2(t)]+3[k_1y_1(t)+k_2y_2(t)]=k_1f_1(t)+k_2f_2(t)
$$

But this equation is the system equation [Eq. (1.43)] with

$$
f(t)=k_1f_1(t)+k_2f_2(t)
$$

and

$$
y(t)=k_1y_1(t)+k_2y_2(t)
$$

Therefore, when the input is $k_1f_1(t)+k_2f_2(t)$, the system response is $k_1y_1(t)+k_2y_2(t)$. Consequently, the system is linear. Using this argument, we can readily generalize the result to show that a system described by a differential equation of the form

$$
\frac{d^ny}{dt^n}+a_{n-1}\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dt^{n-1}}+\cdots+a_0y
=
b_m\frac{d^mf}{dt^m}+\cdots+b_1\frac{df}{dt}+b_0f
$$
\hfill (1.44)

is a linear system. The coefficients $a_i$ and $b_i$ in this equation can be constants or functions of time. ■

More Comments on Linear Systems

Almost all systems observed in practice become nonlinear when large enough signals are applied to them. However, many systems show linear behavior for small signals. The analysis of nonlinear systems is generally difficult. Nonlinearities can arise in so many ways that describing them with a common mathematical form is impossible.
