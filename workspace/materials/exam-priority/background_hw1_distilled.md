## Required Explanation Bias For Background Lessons
When generating `Background` lessons, bias toward the following:
1. Show the canonical forms side-by-side: Cartesian, polar, exponential
2. Use short worked examples with actual numbers, not only concept prose
3. Emphasize the operation rules that make polar form efficient
4. Explain argument / phase carefully, especially sign and quadrant issues
5. For sinusoids, explicitly connect algebraic coefficients to phasor magnitude and angle
6. End each major concept with one exam-style conversion or simplification example

## Required Example Patterns
1. Convert `a + jb` to `r e^{jθ}`
2. Convert `r e^{jθ}` back to `a + jb`
3. Multiply and divide two nontrivial complex numbers
4. Compute a reciprocal using polar form
5. Take a small integer power using polar form
6. Given `F(ω)`, compute real part, imaginary part, magnitude, and angle
7. Rewrite `cos(ωt) + k sin(ωt)` as `A cos(ωt + φ)` or equivalent
8. Rewrite `a cos(ωt) - b sin(ωt)` as a single sinusoid and explain phase sign

## Common Traps To Explicitly Teach
1. Wrong quadrant when computing angle from tangent
2. Forgetting that magnitude is always nonnegative
3. Losing the sign of the imaginary part when converting forms
4. Mixing degree intuition with radian expressions
5. Multiplying Cartesian coordinates termwise instead of using proper complex arithmetic
6. Mishandling division by not subtracting arguments in polar form
7. Forgetting conjugate logic when simplifying rational complex expressions
8. Confusing coefficient matching in sinusoid compression
9. Getting the phase sign wrong when rewriting `a cos + b sin`
10. Reporting only amplitude without the phase-adjusted final sinusoid

## What To De-Emphasize
1. Long historical narrative without computational payoff
2. Purely philosophical discussion of why complex numbers exist
3. Decorative visuals that do not support conversion, phase, or sinusoid combination
4. Proof-heavy treatment when the likely exam skill is procedural manipulation
