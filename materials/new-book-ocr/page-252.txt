3.3-5 Discrete-Time Complex Exponential e^{jΩn}

Using Euler’s formula, we can express an exponential e^{jΩn} in terms of sinusoids as

e^{jΩn} = (cos Ωn + j sin Ωn)      and      e^{-jΩn} = (cos Ωn − j sin Ωn)

These equations show that the frequency of both e^{jΩn} and e^{-jΩn} is Ω (radians/sample). Therefore,
the frequency of e^{jΩn} is |Ω|.
Observe that for r = 1 and θ = nΩ,

e^{jΩn} = re^{jθ}

This equation shows that the magnitude and angle of e^{jΩn} are 1 and nΩ, respectively. In the
complex plane, e^{jΩn} is a point on a unit circle at an angle nΩ.

EXAMPLE 3.5  Plotting a DT Sinusoid with MATLAB

Using MATLAB, plot the discrete-time sinusoid x[n] = cos ((π/12)n + π/4).

We represent the desired sinusoid using an anonymous function. Next, we plot this function
over the desired range of n. The result, shown in Fig. 3.12, matches the plot of the same signal
shown in Fig. 3.11.

>>  n = (-30:30); x = @(n) cos(n*pi/12+pi/4);
>>  clf; stem(n,x(n),'k'); ylabel('x[n]'); xlabel('n');

† Superficially, it may appear that a discrete-time sinusoid is a continuous-time sinusoid’s cousin in a
striped suit. However, some of the properties of discrete-time sinusoids are very different from those of
continuous-time sinusoids. For instance, not every discrete-time sinusoid is periodic. A sinusoid cos Ωn is
periodic only if Ω is a rational multiple of 2π. Also, discrete-time sinusoids are bandlimited to Ω ≤ π.
Any sinusoid with Ω ≥ π can always be expressed as a sinusoid of some frequency Ω ≤ π. These peculiar
properties are the direct consequence of the fact that the period of a discrete-time sinusoid must be an integer.
These topics are discussed in Chs. 5 and 9.
